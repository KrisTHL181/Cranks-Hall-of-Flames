> Derivation of Cosmic Spacetime Characteristic Equations 
>  
>  
>  Define the cosmic characteristic mass parameter: 
>  
>  
>  $k = \frac{M}{R}$ 
>  
>  
>  where M denotes the total mass of visible matter within cosmic spacetime, and R represents the cosmic horizon radius, defined as the light-travel distance from the observer to the central cosmic singularity. 
>  
>  The radial mass density distribution of cosmic spacetime is expressed as: 
>  
>  
>  $\rho(r) = \frac{k}{4\pi r^2}$ 
>  
>  
>  where $\rho(r)$ is the spacetime mass density at radial coordinate r. 
>  
>  Based on the above definitions, three core cosmic spacetime relational equations are derived as follows: 
>  
>  1. Redshift–Distance Relation 
>  
>  The quantitative correspondence between cosmic redshift and observational distance is established as: 
>  
>  
>  $z = \frac{d}{R-d}$ 
>  
>  
>  Equivalently, the distance solving formula is: 
>  
>  
>  $d = \frac{Rz}{1+z}$ 
>  
>  
>  where z denotes the redshift of celestial objects beyond the local supergalaxy, and d stands for the light-travel distance from the observed celestial object to the observer. 
>  
>  2. Energy Density of Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation 
>  
>  The energy density of cosmic microwave background (CMB) radiation satisfies: 
>  
>  
>  $\varepsilon = \frac{c^4}{16\pi^2 R^2 G(1+Z)}$ 
>  
>  
>  where c is the speed of light in vacuum, G is the universal gravitational constant, and Z is the redshift parameter of CMB radiation. 
>  
>  3. Cosmological Constant 
>  
>  The cosmological constant of the static cosmic spacetime is determined as: 
>  
>  
>  $\Lambda = -\frac{2}{R^2}$ 
>  
>  
>  Physical Interpretation 
>  
>  If astronomical observational results are consistent with the theoretical predictions of the above three equations, the cosmic spacetime conforms to the framework of general relativity and possesses a central spacetime singularity. Since human observers and all visible cosmic matter are located inside the horizon of this central singularity, no horizon shielding exists between the singularity and the observers. The singularity is directly observable and is therefore defined as a naked singularity, which constitutes the horizon boundary of the local cosmic spacetime.

>  
>  $k=\frac{1}{2\varepsilon_0\mu_0 G}$
