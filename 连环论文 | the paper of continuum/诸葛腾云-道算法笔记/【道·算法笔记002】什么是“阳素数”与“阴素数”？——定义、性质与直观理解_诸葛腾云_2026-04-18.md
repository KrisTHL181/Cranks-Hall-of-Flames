**一、回顾：模6筛子留下的两条活路**

**在上一篇《模6阴阳分类》中，我们用一个简单的过滤器——模6——把所有大于3的整数过了一遍。结果发现，除了2和3这对异数，任何素数除以6，余数要么是1，要么是5。余0、2、4的偶数被筛掉了，余3的3的倍数也被筛掉了。于是，素数家族被模6这把刀，永久性地分成了两支。**

**今天，我们给这两支正式命名，并深入看看它们的“性格”。**

**二、定义：阳素数（6k+1）与阴素数（6k-1）**

**定义1（阳素数）：若素数p>3满足p≡1(mod6)，即p=6k+1（k为正整数），则称p为阳素数。**

**定义2（阴素数）：若素数p>3满足p≡5(mod6)，即p=6k-1（k为正整数），则称p为阴素数。**

**由于5≡-1(mod6)，阴素数也常写作p=6k-1，更显对称之美。**

**阳素数举例：7, 13, 19, 31, 37, 43, 61, 67, 73, 79…**

**阴素数举例：5, 11, 17, 23, 29, 41, 47, 53, 59, 71…**

**注意，2和3不参与此分类，它们是素数家族的“始祖”，超越阴阳。**

**三、直观性质：阳与阴的“性格”差异**

**如果只是给个名字，这分类便毫无意义。阳素数与阴素数在数论中确实表现出不同的“行为偏斜”。**

**性质1（切比雪夫偏斜）：在不超过x的素数中，阴素数的个数似乎总比阳素数“略多”一点。这种现象被称为“切比雪夫偏斜”。虽然当x趋于无穷时两者比例趋近于1:1，但在有限范围内，阴素数总是略微占优。有人戏称：“阴盛阳衰”。**

**性质2（孪生素数对）：一对孪生素数（相差2的素数对），除了(3,5)这对，其余的必然是一个阴素数和一个阳素数。且较小的那个必为阴素数（6k-1），较大的必为阳素数（6k+1）。例如(5,7)、(11,13)、(17,19)。这是模6分类的一个漂亮推论。**

**性质3（二次剩余特征）：对于模6分类，阳素数与阴素数在模某些特定素数Q下的二次剩余表现，有规律可循。这正是后续“压缩算子”构造权函数的关键，此处暂不展开。**

**四、下篇预告**

**有了阴阳素数的定义，下一步自然是看看它们的“乘积”——半素数——会呈现怎样的阴阳属性。这正是哥德巴赫猜想中“污染项”的来源。下一篇《半素数的阴阳分解：哥德巴赫猜想的“污染项”从何而来》，我们将深入这一核心。**

**Abstract**

**Following the introduction of the "Modulo-6 Yin-Yang Classification" in the previous article, this paper formally defines Yang primes and Yin primes. Through concrete examples and property analysis, it aims to build a solid intuition for this binary classification. Core parameters are withheld; this document serves solely as a public declaration of the original conceptual framework.**

**1. Review: The Two Survival Paths Left by the Modulo-6 Sieve**

**In the previous article, Modulo-6 Yin-Yang Classification, we passed all integers greater than 3 through a simple filter: modulo 6. The result was that, apart from the outliers 2 and 3, every prime leaves a remainder of either 1 or 5 upon division by 6. Remainders 0, 2, and 4 (even numbers) were sieved out, as was remainder 3 (multiples of 3). Thus, the family of primes was permanently split into two branches by the modulo-6 knife.**

**Today, we formally name these two branches and examine their "character" more closely.**

**2. Definitions: Yang Primes (6k+1) and Yin Primes (6k-1)**

**Definition 1 (Yang Prime): A prime p > 3 satisfying p ≡ 1 (mod 6), i.e., p = 6k+1 for some positive integer k, is called a Yang prime.**

**Definition 2 (Yin Prime): A prime p > 3 satisfying p ≡ 5 (mod 6), i.e., p = 6k-1 for some positive integer k, is called a Yin prime.**

**Since 5 ≡ -1 (mod 6), Yin primes are often written as p = 6k-1, which highlights the symmetry.**

**Examples of Yang primes: 7, 13, 19, 31, 37, 43, 61, 67, 73, 79…**

**Examples of Yin primes: 5, 11, 17, 23, 29, 41, 47, 53, 59, 71…**

**Note that 2 and 3 are excluded from this classification; they are the "ancestors" of the prime family, transcending yin and yang.**

**3. Intuitive Properties: The "Personality" Differences Between Yang and Yin**

**A classification is meaningless without consequences. Yang primes and Yin primes do indeed exhibit distinct behavioral biases in number theory.**

**Property 1 (Chebyshev's Bias): Among primes not exceeding x, the count of Yin primes consistently appears slightly larger than that of Yang primes. This phenomenon is known as "Chebyshev's bias." Although the ratio approaches 1:1 as x tends to infinity, Yin primes always hold a slight lead in finite ranges. One might jokingly say: "Yin prevails over Yang."**

**Property 2 (Twin Primes): Except for the pair (3,5), every pair of twin primes (primes differing by 2) consists of one Yin prime and one Yang prime. Moreover, the smaller one is invariably a Yin prime (6k-1), and the larger one a Yang prime (6k+1). Examples include (5,7), (11,13), and (17,19). This is an elegant corollary of the modulo-6 classification.**

**Property 3 (Quadratic Residue Character): Under the modulo-6 classification, the quadratic residue behavior of Yang and Yin primes modulo certain fixed primes Q follows discernible patterns. This is precisely the key to constructing the weight function for the "compression operator," to be discussed in future installments.**
