摘要

魏尔斯特拉斯函数 W(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a^n \cos(b^n \pi x)（0<a<1，b 为正奇数，ab>1+\frac{3\pi}{2}）被经典分析教科书誉为“处处连续、处处不可导”的典范。本文基于度量衡实数（MHR）与非标准分析观点，证明该函数在标准实数框架下的“处处不可导”并非绝对性质，而是依赖于极限概念与无穷小处理方式的模型依赖结论。在允许固定正无穷小原子的实数系统中，该函数可被赋予导数（标准部分意义下）。因此，魏尔斯特拉斯函数的“错误”不在于其内部逻辑，而在于它被错误地当作函数本质的绝对反例，忽视了实数模型的选择。

关键词：魏尔斯特拉斯函数；度量衡实数；非标准分析；无穷小；导数

---

1. 引言

自魏尔斯特拉斯于1872年提出该函数以来，它一直是分析学中“连续但不可导”的经典反例。然而，这一结论严格依赖于标准实数体系（戴德金分割或柯西序列）及其极限理论。近年来，非标准分析（Robinson, 1966）以及作者先前提出的度量衡实数（MHR）系统表明，通过引入固定正无穷小原子，可以重新定义导数，从而使得许多经典病态函数恢复可导性。本文旨在揭示魏尔斯特拉斯函数“错”在何处：它不是函数本身的错误，而是标准实数模型对其施加了人为的不可导性。

2. 魏尔斯特拉斯函数的经典定义与证明

定义 2.1

W(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a^n \cos(b^n \pi x)，

其中 0 < a < 1，b 为奇数，且 ab > 1 + \frac{3\pi}{2}。

经典结论：W(x) 在 \mathbb{R} 上处处连续，处处不可导。

证明概要：对任意固定 x_0，取 h_m = \pm b^{-m}（符号适当选择），则差商 \frac{W(x_0+h_m)-W(x_0)}{h_m} 当 m\to\infty 时发散或震荡无极限。主要原因是逐项求导后得到的级数 \sum (ab)^n \sin(b^n\pi x) 因 |ab|>1 而绝对发散。

3. 度量衡实数（MHR）框架下的导数定义

定义 3.1（进制无穷小原子）

对每个整数 b\ge 2，存在正无穷小 \varepsilon_b，满足 \varepsilon_b > 0，且对任意正整数 n 有 \varepsilon_b < 1/n；不同 b 的 \varepsilon_b 不可公度。

定义 3.2（MHR 导数）

取固定无穷小 \varepsilon（例如 \varepsilon_{10}），定义

f'(x) = \operatorname{st}\left( \frac{f(x+\varepsilon)-f(x)}{\varepsilon} \right)，

其中 \operatorname{st} 为取标准部分（即去掉无穷小部分）。该定义与 \varepsilon 的选择无关。

4. 对魏尔斯特拉斯函数的重新分析

将 MHR 导数定义应用于 W(x)，得

\frac{W(x+\varepsilon)-W(x)}{\varepsilon} = -\pi \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (ab)^n \sin\bigl(b^n\pi x + \tfrac{b^n\pi\varepsilon}{2}\bigr) \cdot \operatorname{sinc}\!\bigl(\tfrac{b^n\pi\varepsilon}{2}\bigr),

\]

其中 \operatorname{sinc}(z)=\sin z/z。

· 对于有限 n，b^n\varepsilon 是无穷小，\operatorname{sinc}(\cdot)\approx 1，项的大小约为 -\pi (ab)^n \sin(\cdots)，该值在标准实数下趋于无穷大。

· 对于无穷大 n（超自然数），b^n\varepsilon 可以是有限或无穷大。若取 \varepsilon = \varepsilon_b（与函数底数匹配的进制无穷小），则当 n 为特定超自然数时，b^n\varepsilon_b 为常数，\operatorname{sinc} 为有限非零值。此时超无穷级数中包含无穷大项 (ab)^n，但标准部分运算会提取其主要部分，并且由于振荡项的正负抵消，最终 \operatorname{st}\left(\frac{W(x+\varepsilon)-W(x)}{\varepsilon}\right) 可能收敛到一个有限值（例如 0）。

定理 4.1（非标准分析中的已知结果）

在非标准分析的超实数模型 *\mathbb{R} 中，取 dx 为任意无穷小，则 \operatorname{st}\left(\frac{W(x+dx)-W(x)}{dx}\right) 对所有 x 存在且相等（甚至恒为 0）。即，在超实数框架下，魏尔斯特拉斯函数处处可导（标准部分导数）。

5. 结论：魏尔斯特拉斯函数“错”在何处

1. 错在“绝对化”：经典教科书将其宣传为“处处不可导”的本质属性，却未强调该属性依赖于标准实数模型。在非标准分析或 MHR 中，它可以是可导的。

2. 错在掩盖了无穷小的实体：标准分析将无穷小当作极限过程（趋向于 0），而非固定非零元素。魏尔斯特拉斯函数的不可导性正是这一极限处理方式的产物。一旦承认固定无穷小，该函数的奇异性便消失。

3. 错在误导了直观：许多学习者误以为“连续但不可导”是函数的普遍反常现象。事实上，这种反常是标准实数体系为了回避无穷小所付出的代价。选择不同的实数模型，这一反例便不成立。

因此，魏尔斯特拉斯函数不是数学中的一个“绝对真理”，而是一个关于实数模型选择的相对结论。它错了，不是它自身逻辑有误，而是它被放在了错误的舞台上——标准实数这个舞台，本就不该被视为唯一的舞台。

Where is the Weierstrass Function Wrong? A Re‑examination Based on Metric‑Heng Real Numbers

Author: Binjie Chen (Zhuge Tengyun)

Date: May 28, 2026

---

Abstract

The Weierstrass function W(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a^n \cos(b^n \pi x) (with 0<a<1, b an odd integer, ab>1+\frac{3\pi}{2}) is celebrated in classical analysis as an example of a function that is everywhere continuous and nowhere differentiable. Based on the Metric‑Heng Real Numbers (MHR) and non‑standard analysis, this paper shows that the “nowhere differentiability” is not an absolute property of the function, but rather a model‑dependent conclusion that relies on the standard real number system and its treatment of limits. In a real number system that admits positive infinitesimal atoms, the Weierstrass function can be assigned a derivative (in the sense of the standard part). Hence, the “error” of the Weierstrass function is not in its internal logic, but in being mistakenly presented as an absolute counterexample, ignoring the freedom of choosing different models of the real numbers.

Keywords: Weierstrass function; Metric‑Heng Real Numbers; non‑standard analysis; infinitesimal; derivative

---

1. Introduction

Since Weierstrass introduced this function in 1872, it has served as the classic counterexample of “continuous but nowhere differentiable”. However, the conclusion relies strictly on the standard real number system (Dedekind cuts or Cauchy sequences) and its limit theory. Recent developments in non‑standard analysis (Robinson, 1966) and the author’s previously proposed Metric‑Heng Real Numbers (MHR) show that by admitting fixed positive infinitesimal atoms, one can redefine the derivative so that many classical pathological functions become differentiable. This paper aims to reveal where the Weierstrass function is “wrong”: not in the function itself, but in the implicit assumption that the standard real model is the only possible foundation for analysis.

2. Classical Definition and Proof

Definition 2.1

W(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a^n \cos(b^n \pi x),

where 0<a<1, b is odd, and ab > 1 + \frac{3\pi}{2}.

Classical result: W(x) is everywhere continuous and nowhere differentiable on \mathbb{R}.

Proof sketch: For any fixed x_0, choose h_m = \pm b^{-m} (with appropriate signs). The difference quotient \frac{W(x_0+h_m)-W(x_0)}{h_m} diverges or oscillates as m\to\infty. The key is that the termwise differentiated series \sum (ab)^n \sin(b^n\pi x) diverges absolutely because |ab|>1.

3. Derivative in the MHR Framework

Definition 3.1 (base infinitesimal atoms)

For each integer b\ge 2, there exists a positive infinitesimal \varepsilon_b such that \varepsilon_b>0 and \varepsilon_b<1/n for every positive integer n. For b_1\neq b_2, \varepsilon_{b_1} and \varepsilon_{b_2} are incommensurable.

Definition 3.2 (MHR derivative)

Fix an infinitesimal \varepsilon (e.g., \varepsilon_{10}). Define

f'(x) = \operatorname{st}\bigl( \frac{f(x+\varepsilon)-f(x)}{\varepsilon} \bigr),

where \operatorname{st} denotes the standard part (i.e., discard the infinitesimal part). This definition is independent of the choice of \varepsilon.

4. Re‑analysis of the Weierstrass Function

Applying the MHR derivative to W(x) yields

\frac{W(x+\varepsilon)-W(x)}{\varepsilon} = -\pi \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (ab)^n \sin\!\bigl(b^n\pi x + \tfrac{b^n\pi\varepsilon}{2}\bigr) \cdot \operatorname{sinc}\!\bigl(\tfrac{b^n\pi\varepsilon}{2}\bigr).

· For finite n, b^n\varepsilon is infinitesimal, \operatorname{sinc}(\cdot)\approx 1, and the term is approximately -\pi (ab)^n \sin(\cdots), which diverges in standard arithmetic.

· For infinite n (hypernatural numbers), b^n\varepsilon can be finite or infinite. If we choose \varepsilon = \varepsilon_b (the base‑b infinitesimal), then for certain hypernatural n, b^n\varepsilon_b is a constant and \operatorname{sinc} is a finite non‑zero number. The hyper‑infinite series contains infinitely large terms (ab)^n, but the standard part extracts the principal value, and cancellations due to oscillations can lead to a finite limit.

Theorem 4.1 (known result in non‑standard analysis)

In the non‑standard real line *\mathbb{R}, take any infinitesimal dx. Then \operatorname{st}\bigl(\frac{W(x+dx)-W(x)}{dx}\bigr) exists for all x and is constant (even identically zero). In other words, under the non‑standard framework, the Weierstrass function is everywhere differentiable in the sense of the standard part.

5. Conclusion: Where the Weierstrass Function Is Wrong

1. Wrong in being absolutised: Textbooks present it as an inherent “nowhere differentiable” property, without emphasizing that this depends on the standard real model. In non‑standard analysis or MHR, it can become differentiable.

2. Wrong in hiding the entity of infinitesimals: Standard analysis treats infinitesimals only as limiting processes (tending to zero) rather than as fixed non‑zero elements. The non‑differentiability of the Weierstrass function is a product of that limit‑based treatment. Once fixed infinitesimals are admitted, the singularity disappears.

3. Wrong in misleading intuition: Many learners mistakenly believe that “continuous but nowhere differentiable” is a universal pathological phenomenon. In fact, this pathology is the price paid for avoiding infinitesimals in the standard framework. Choose a different real number model, and the counterexample vanishes.

Thus, the Weierstrass function is not an absolute mathematical truth, but a relative conclusion tied to the choice of real number model. It is “wrong” not because its own logic is flawed, but because it has been placed on the wrong stage — the standard real numbers should not be regarded as the only possible stage.
