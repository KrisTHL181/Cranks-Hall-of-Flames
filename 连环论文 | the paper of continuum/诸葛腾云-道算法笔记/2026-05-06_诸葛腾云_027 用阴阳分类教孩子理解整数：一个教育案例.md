摘要

数学教育的痛点在于抽象。本文分享一个真实教学案例：如何用模6阴阳分类，让小学生直观理解素数、合数、因数分解。阴阳分类将整数变成“看得见”的两种颜色，孩子在分类游戏中自然习得了数论的基本思想。这是“道”在教育现场的一次落地。

一、教学场景

对象：小学五年级学生，已学因数与倍数。痛点：素数判定靠死记，不理解“为什么”。工具：一张百数表，两种颜色彩笔。

二、模6分类游戏

1. 让孩子将1~100所有数除以6，写出余数。

2. 余1涂红色（阳），余5涂蓝色（阴），其余涂灰色。

3. 引导观察：大于3的素数，全都在红色或蓝色格里。为什么？

4. 孩子自己发现：灰色格里，余0、2、4的都是偶数，余3的都是3的倍数。所以素数只能待在红蓝格里。

三、半素数“破案”游戏

给出几个数：25、35、49、55。问：它们都在红蓝格里，是素数吗？孩子计算发现都不是。进一步分解：25=5×5（蓝×蓝→红），35=5×7（蓝×红→蓝）。引导总结：两个素数乘起来，颜色会“混合”——这就是半素数的阴阳分解雏形。

四、教学效果与反思

课后，孩子自发提问：“有没有永远猜不出颜色的数？”——这正是数论中随机性与决定论的深刻问题。阴阳分类将抽象的模运算变成了视觉游戏，降低了认知门槛。更重要的，孩子在“分类-观察-猜想-验证”中，体验了数学家的工作流程。这正是五维螺旋在教育中的微观映射：B（分类边界）→F（颜色资源）→G（发现规律）→C（反例挑战）→A（内化为直觉）。

Abstract

The pain point of mathematics education is abstraction. This article shares a real teaching case: how to use modulo-6 yin-yang classification to help elementary school students intuitively understand primes, composites, and factorization. Yin-yang classification turns integers into two "visible" colors; through classification games, children naturally internalize fundamental ideas of number theory. This is "Tao" landing in the educational field.

1. Teaching Context

Audience: fifth graders who have learned factors and multiples. Pain point: prime identification relies on rote memorization without understanding "why." Tools: a hundred chart, two colored markers.

2. The Modulo-6 Classification Game

1. Ask the child to divide each number from 1 to 100 by 6 and write down the remainder.

2. Color remainder 1 red (Yang), remainder 5 blue (Yin), others gray.

3. Guide observation: primes greater than 3 all lie in red or blue cells. Why?

4. The child discovers independently: in gray cells, remainders 0,2,4 are even numbers; remainder 3 are multiples of 3. So primes can only stay in red/blue cells.

3. The Semiprime "Detective" Game

Present numbers like 25, 35, 49, 55. Ask: they are in red/blue cells—are they prime? The child computes and finds they are not. Further factorization: 25=5×5 (blue×blue→red), 35=5×7 (blue×red→blue). Guide to summarize: multiplying two primes "mixes" their colors—this is the embryo of yin-yang decomposition of semiprimes.

4. Pedagogical Effect and Reflection

After class, the child spontaneously asked: "Is there a number whose color you can never guess?"—a profound question touching the boundary between randomness and determinism in number theory. Yin-yang classification turned abstract modular arithmetic into a visual game, lowering the cognitive barrier. More importantly, through "classify–observe–conjecture–verify," the child experienced the workflow of a mathematician. This is a microcosm of the Five-Dimension Spiral in education: B (classification boundary) → F (color resources) → G (pattern discovery) → C (counterexample challenge) → A (internalized intuition).
