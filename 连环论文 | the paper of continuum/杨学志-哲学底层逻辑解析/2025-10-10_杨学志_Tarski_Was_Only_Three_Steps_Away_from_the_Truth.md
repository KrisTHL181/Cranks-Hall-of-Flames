Alfred Tarski (1901-1983) was one of the most influential logicians, mathematicians, and philosophers of the 20th century. Born in Poland and later emigrating to the United States, his core research focused on “analyzing the meaning of language using rigorous logical and mathematical methods.” His theory of truth (the theory of truth definition) is hailed as the cornerstone of modern logical semantics, revolutionizing the traditional vague discussions about “truth.” UNESCO designated January 14 as World Logic Day to commemorate two great logicians: Kurt Gödel and Alfred Tarski.

## Tarski’s Theory of Truth

Traditional philosophical discussions about “truth” often fell into vagueness (e.g., claims like “truth corresponds to facts”). Tarski’s goal was to provide a “formally rigorous and substantively reasonable” definition of “truth” to avoid logical contradictions (such as the “ liar paradox ”). The core of his theory can be summarized in two key points:

### 1. Core Definition: The T-schema (Anchoring “Truth”)

Tarski argued that the “truth” of a sentence essentially lies in “the consistency between what the sentence describes and the actual state of affairs.” He formalized this relationship through the “T-schema”: *“A sentence P is true if and only if P holds in reality”* .

Examples include:

- *“‘Snow is white’ is true if and only if snow is white”* 

- *“‘2 + 2 = 4’ is true if and only if 2 + 2 = 4”* 


It is important to clarify:

- The left side ( *“‘Snow is white’ is true”* ) is an evaluation of the “truth value of the sentence itself,” focusing on judging “whether the statement is correct”; 

- The right side ( *“Snow is white”* ) refers to “the actual state of affairs described by the sentence,” focusing on stating “what the fact is”; 

- The phrase “if and only if” (a logical connective) clarifies the complete equivalence between the two sides: the “truth” of a sentence is essentially equivalent to the fulfillment of the corresponding state of affairs in reality, leaving no room for additional vague interpretations. 


### 2. Key Premise: The Hierarchy of Languages (Avoiding Paradoxes)

Traditional discussions about “truth” were prone to the “liar paradox” (e.g., *“This sentence I am now saying is false”* : if the sentence is true, it implies it is false; if it is false, it implies it is true). Through analysis, Tarski identified that the root cause of such paradoxes lies in “self-reference of language”—that is, “using the same level of language both to describe reality and to evaluate its own truth or falsity” (semantic closure).

To solve this problem, he proposed the “hierarchy of languages” theory:

- **Object language** : Only used to describe the actual state of affairs and does not involve evaluations of “truth or falsity,” e.g., “Snow is white” or “I am speaking”; 

- **Meta-language** : Used to evaluate the truth or falsity of the “object language,” e.g., *“‘Snow is white’ is true”* or *“‘I am speaking’ is false”* ; 

- To evaluate the truth or falsity of the “meta-language,” a higher-level “meta-meta-language” is required, forming a progressive chain of language hierarchies. 


This hierarchy resolves the liar paradox: the statement *“This sentence I am now saying is false”* attempts to use the same level of language to act as both the “object language” (stating content) and the “meta-language” (evaluating truth or falsity). However, the hierarchy of languages requires that “evaluation must be conducted in a higher-level language,” thus completely avoiding self-reference in language.

Later evaluations of Tarski do not focus on claiming he “invented truth,” but rather recognize his achievement in “transforming ‘truth’ from a vague philosophical concept into a scientific concept that can be rigorously analyzed through logic and mathematics.”

Nevertheless, Tarski’s theory was still three steps away from “the essence of truth”:

## 1. Failure to Correctly Understand the Nature of the Liar Paradox

The typical form of the liar paradox is *“This sentence is false”* , but the sentence itself is not a paradox. It is only misinterpreted as a paradox, and this misinterpretation essentially violates the basic laws of logic.

The “law of identity” in logic requires that a linguistic symbol must uniquely refer to an “object with identity.” Although this logical law was not accurately expressed in the traditional “three laws of logic” and was only refined in the enhanced logical laws I proposed, all successful theories have already adhered to this principle.

For example, if the symbol “y” refers to both 1 and 2 simultaneously, then according to the identity “y = y,” it would absurdly imply 1 = 2. In a class where two students are both named “Zhang Jun,” the teacher would distinguish them as “Big Zhang Jun” and “Small Zhang Jun” to avoid confusion.

Similarly, the phrase “this sentence” in *“This sentence is false”* must also uniquely refer to one specific sentence. Suppose “this sentence” refers to *“Snow is white”* ; then *“This sentence is false”* would be equivalent to *“‘Snow is white’ is false”* . Therefore, when the law of identity is observed, self-reference cannot occur. Self-reference is merely a product of misinterpretation rooted in logical errors.

## 2. The T-schema Is a Tautology

This issue can be discussed from two perspectives:

### Positive Perspective

According to Tarski’s theory, *“‘Snow is white’ is true if and only if snow is white”* .

However, the key question is: How do we determine whether the condition after “if and only if” holds?

In fact, when we write the words “Snow is white” on paper, its inherent meaning is *“The proposition ‘Snow is white’ is true”* . Similarly, when we write “Snow is black” on paper, its meaning is *“The proposition ‘Snow is black’ is true”* . This is a fundamental requirement of language itself.

Tarski’s definition is not a definition at all—it is merely a tautology.

### Negative Perspective

Tarski’s T-sentence is interpreted as: *“The proposition ‘Snow is white’ is true if and only if ‘Snow is white’ holds in reality”* .

Yet, “Snow is white” is a statement made by a person. What force guarantees that this person’s statement aligns with reality?

Therefore, Tarski’s T-sentence should be revised to: *“‘Snow is white’ is true if and only if it is stated by a person whose words correspond to facts”* —and such a person would be “God.”

If Tarski were not God, there would be a logical leap from “language” to “fact.”

If this leap is denied, then *“The proposition ‘Snow is white’ is true”* and *“‘Snow is white’ holds in reality”* become a tautology.

## 3. The Hierarchy of Languages Originates from the Distinction Between Cognition and Practice

Tarski introduced the hierarchy of languages to avoid self-reference and resolve the liar paradox. However, as we have argued earlier, self-reference is a logical error that can be resolved solely by adhering to logical laws.

Tarski’s introduction of the new concept of “language hierarchy” to solve the liar paradox—if one has a thorough understanding of the history of science—should raise concerns that this approach has already (or at least potentially) gone astray.

In traditional physics, it was believed that wave propagation required a medium: for example, sound waves propagate through media such as air, water, or railway tracks. After physics confirmed that light is a type of wave, a question arose: What medium allows light to travel from the Sun to the Earth? At that time, it was already known that most of the space between the Sun and the Earth is a vacuum (with no air).

How, then, to explain light’s propagation from the Sun to the Earth? What was the medium?

Some physicists introduced a new concept: “ether”—a colorless, odorless, massless substance that does not impede the movement of tangible matter, is absolutely rigid, and enables light propagation. However, the later Michelson-Morley experiment disproved the existence of ether. In contrast, Einstein’s theory of relativity successfully explained the Michelson-Morley experiment by only revising existing theory (replacing Galilean transformations with Lorentz transformation s) without introducing any new concepts.

Science follows a fundamental principle known as “ Occam’s Razor ,” traditionally stated as *“Entities should not be multiplied beyond necessity”* . This principle, also called the “principle of parsimony in thought,” requires explaining phenomena using the fewest possible concepts and expressions. Recklessly introducing new concepts is a sign of “squandering thought” and insufficient thinking ability.

Tarski’s hierarchy of languages is precisely the introduction of an unnecessary new concept. Humans have been using language for tens of thousands of years, yet never before has anyone claimed that language needs to be divided into multiple layers. For someone to suddenly propose such a hierarchy and call it a “great discovery” is questionable.

To be clear, my criticism of Tarski here is intentionally provocative. In fact, his hierarchy of languages is not entirely baseless—but he failed to understand its true rationale and arbitrarily forced it to explain phenomena, coincidentally achieving some valid results. This is analogous to the Lorentz transformation: the transformation itself was not invented by Einstein; its creator, Hendrik Lorentz, was a contemporary of Einstein and 26 years his senior. Although Einstein used the Lorentz transformation to propose the theory of relativity, the theory is not attributed to Lorentz—because Lorentz did not support the theory of relativity in its early stages.

Tarski’s hierarchy of languages suffered from two flaws: first, its motivation was incorrect; second, it introduced unnecessary new concepts. Nevertheless, his work was a beneficial attempt, even if it was a case of “stumbling into success by accident.”

Most people are familiar with the saying: *“Understanding the world is for the sake of transforming the world”* . This statement contains two implications:

1. **Understanding the world** : The theories related to this process are called “epistemology,” and the propositions within it are “epistemological propositions,” which describe *how the world is* ; 

2. **Transforming the world** : The theories related to this process include “axiology” (the study of value) and “praxeology” (the study of practice). Based on our understanding of the world, we evaluate the role and value of various forms of knowledge—this is axiology. We then use these evaluations to guide our actions—this is praxeology. Axiology and praxeology are generally two consecutive processes, but they can also be viewed as a single unified process. Even in academic research, discussing value without considering practice still aims to provide guidance for future practice. 


Let us take an example: 
 You are surrounded by many objects—such as a mobile phone, a book, braised pork, rice, and dumbbells. Recognizing these objects falls under the scope of epistemology. Pointing to your mobile phone and saying *“This is a mobile phone”* is an epistemological proposition.

Epistemological propositions ultimately reduce to sensory judgments. To determine whether the statement *“This is a mobile phone”* is correct, you ultimately rely on sensory perceptions of the object: its size, dimensions, structure, whether it can make calls, and whether you can hear the other person’s voice through the receiver.

In contrast, **axiological propositions** involve a subject’s evaluation, value judgment, or more simply, “scoring” of an object.

Evaluations are subjective and private. Their results guide the subject’s actions. Axiological propositions have no “truth or falsity”—only “support or opposition,” and their validity is determined by the outcomes of practice.

Suppose you have recognized the objects around you and are currently feeling hungry. To address this situation, you need to “score” the objects you have identified. You might give braised pork a score of 10, rice a score of 3, and the other objects a score of 0. Your subsequent action (practice) would be to eat a bowl of rice with 8 pieces of braised pork. If you have high blood pressure, your choice might be a bowl of rice with 1 piece of braised pork.

Imagine if someone chose two dumbbells instead—the result of their practice would be broken teeth followed by starvation. Over time, no one would repeat such a choice.

The “truth” that Tarski was obsessed with is essentially a subject’s axiological judgment of an epistemological proposition, which serves as the basis for their practice. Therefore, it requires no additional definition.

If a person judges that the proposition *“Braised pork is a substance that sustains life”* is “true,” and they feel hungry (worried about their survival), they will eat braised pork. If they believe dumbbells have this function and try to chew them, the outcome is predictable. Everyone has their own judgments of “truth or falsity,” which explains the ups and downs of the diverse world we observe.

Within the framework of epistemology and axiology, propositions are also divided into two layers. In terms of hierarchy alone, this is similar to Tarski’s distinction between the object language layer and the meta-language layer.

However, this superficial similarity masks an essential difference: In Tarski’s theory of language hierarchy, the hierarchy is a “property of language”—artificially defined by Tarski to solve the liar paradox through the introduction of a new concept. In contrast, the distinction between epistemology and axiology arises from “two stages of the human practice process”: one stage is understanding the world, and the other is transforming the world. Understanding the world is the foundation for transforming the world. These two stages are indispensable—neither can be omitted.

## Conclusion

Tarski failed to recognize that the liar paradox is essentially a logical error; his T-schema is a tautology; and his hierarchy of languages only bears a formal similarity to the system of epistemology and axiology. After resolving these three issues, the structure of human knowledge is finally clarified.
