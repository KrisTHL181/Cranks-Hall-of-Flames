Identity is the capability and characteristic of a thing being itself and not influenced by the observer.

Identity is the necessary condition for a thing to be a valid object of thought. A subject need time to observe and think about a thing to get a conclusion about it. The thing must keep being itself and do not change to another one during the period in which the subject is observing and thinking to get the conclusion, which otherwise will be obsolete and invalid.

A thing with identity can be referred to as a symbol.

A set with identity can be expressed by a symbol, such as Q. If Q is an element of a set, which can be expressed by {Q, ...}. If we admit the  effect of the braces can not be counteracted by the other elements of the set, then we get Q!=  {Q, ...}. That is, for any Q , $Q \notin Q$ .

The set $Q=\{x| x \notin x\}$ is actually the set of all sets, since every set x satisfies $x \notin x$ .  Noticeably, I omit the adjective modifier "with identity" behind "all sets", since any object of thought should have identity, by default. So, even though Q satisfies $Q \notin Q$ , this property can not be applied to Q's criteria of definition to get $Q \in Q$ , because $Q \notin Q$ is the property after its definition and getting its identity.

Then Russell's paradox is solved by the concept of identity.
